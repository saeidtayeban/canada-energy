Key finding

Freight is the second-largest energy consumer and the fastest-growing sector, up 10.3% over the decade. Light trucks and heavy trucks together account for the bulk of that demand — and neither electrifies cheaply or easily.

Who's emitting, and how much

Total Transportation's GHG figure includes all sub-sectors, making it the single largest emissions source in the dataset at 2,010,626 units. Passenger vehicles and freight trucks together explain why transportation is Canada's largest source of greenhouse gas emissions nationally.

Trend · 2013–2023

A decade of divergence

Overall energy use rose +3.7% from 2013 to 2023. But that headline masks a split: freight grew aggressively while electricity infrastructure actually declined slightly.

Sector growth 2013 → 2023
Freight ↑ 10.3%

The most concerning trend in the dataset. Freight growth tracks e-commerce expansion and supply chain intensification — a structural shift, not a blip. Without electrification, this keeps climbing.

Electricity ↓ 0.4%

A marginal decline in electricity sector energy use is a quiet positive signal — efficiency gains and grid optimization are having a measurable effect, even if small.

Clean energy share trend — 2013 to 2023
The COVID distortion

Clean energy share spiked to 22.1% in 2020 — not because Canada got cleaner, but because overall demand collapsed during COVID-19. By 2023 it had fallen back to 19.4%, lower than any year since 2013. Demand is outpacing clean supply.

GHG Intensity · 2013–2023

The grid is cleaning up.
The roads are not.

GHG intensity measures emissions per unit of energy — a proxy for how clean each sector's energy source is getting over time.

Electricity
−23.1%
The single strongest signal in the dataset. Canada's grid is meaningfully decarbonizing — renewables and nuclear are displacing fossil generation.
Passenger
+0.6%
Essentially flat. Fuel efficiency gains in cars and SUVs are being cancelled out by the shift toward larger, heavier vehicles and more kilometres driven.
Freight
+2.5%
Moving in the wrong direction. Heavier loads, longer hauls, and minimal EV penetration in commercial trucking means freight intensity is getting worse, not better.
The core tension

Canada's electricity grid is on a genuine decarbonization trajectory. Its transportation network is not. Chapter 2 models what happens when you connect these two systems — pushing EV adoption onto a grid that isn't ready for the load.
