EV Share · 2024

Two provinces are pulling away

British Columbia and Quebec lead the country in EV adoption — both well above 7%. Most of the rest of the country sits clustered between 2–3%, with Saskatchewan trailing at 1.5%.

EV share of total registrations by province — 2024
Key finding

BC (7.9%) and Quebec (7.0%) are running roughly 5x ahead of Saskatchewan (1.5%). Both leaders combine strong provincial purchase incentives with hydro-dominant grids — cheap, clean power makes EV ownership economically rational in a way it isn't yet in fossil-heavy provinces.

Vehicle Segments

Trucks and SUVs are already the majority

Of 1.27M registered EVs in 2024, light trucks and SUVs outnumber passenger cars — the segment that consumes 30–40% more energy per charge. Heavy freight and urban transit remain almost entirely unelectrified in the registration data itself.

Data gap, handled

Heavy freight and transit EV counts are near-zero in StatCan's registration tables — not a data error, but a real reflection of how early these segments are. The demand model substitutes federal Medium- and Heavy-Duty Vehicle ZEV targets (35% of new MHDV sales by 2030) as the input for freight, since registration history alone can't support a projection yet.

Policy · September 2025 – April 2026

The mandate moved while the model was being built

In September 2025 the federal government paused the 2026 ZEV sales mandate and opened a 60-day review. By early 2026, both the federal target and British Columbia's had been revised down from 100% to 75% by 2035. Quebec moved earlier, cutting its target to 90%.

100% → 90%
The smallest rollback of the three — and the earliest, moving before the federal review concluded. Near-term targets unchanged.
Why this matters for the model

A single adoption curve can't capture this anymore. Every projection in this chapter runs in two parallel scenarios — original (pre-pause) and revised (post-rollback) — so the gap between them becomes a measurable quantity rather than an assumption buried in the model.

The gap that matters

The mandate rollback shaves roughly 1.6 TWh off national incremental demand by 2035 — about 7% less growth. That's real, but small relative to the ~20 TWh of new load arriving either way. The physical grid stress this chapter measures doesn't meaningfully change based on which policy version wins.

Grid Stress Score · 2035, Revised Scenario

The Prairies and the Maritimes carry the load

Stress score measures incremental EV demand as a share of each province's available grid headroom. Quebec and Newfoundland — both hydro-dominant — absorb the new load easily. Five mid-sized, fossil-leaning grids do not.

* Prince Edward Island excluded from the ranking above. Its 1.5 TWh grid is small enough that percentage-based stress scores are distorted by scale, and PEI imports roughly 60% of its electricity from New Brunswick — its own generation capacity isn't the binding constraint. PEI's raw score (87.4%) is reported separately rather than blended into the provincial ranking.
The stressed cluster

Saskatchewan, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Manitoba, and Alberta cluster tightly between 43.5% and 48.8% — not coincidence. All five run smaller-to-mid grids with limited hydro and heavier reliance on fossil generation, leaving less headroom to absorb new EV load than their population size would suggest.

The hydro advantage

Quebec (16.9%) and Newfoundland (7.1%) sit far below the rest of the country — both built on hydro capacity large relative to their population. Ontario (26.8%) lands in between: the largest grid in the country, but also the largest absolute EV fleet.
