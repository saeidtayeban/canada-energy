Province selection

Two provinces. Maximum contrast.

Ontario and Alberta were chosen because they have the best open hourly grid data in Canada — and because they tell opposite stories. Ontario's nuclear-and-hydro grid is one of the cleanest in North America. Alberta runs on gas and coal. Smart charging has the same operational effect in both provinces. The climate impact is 16× larger in Alberta.

Charging model

Dumb charging creates the problem.
Smart charging eliminates it.

The model converts Chapter 2's annual EV demand projections (TWh) into hourly MW load profiles using two charging behaviour assumptions. Light vehicles follow home charging patterns. Heavy freight charges at depots overnight regardless of scenario.

Dumb charging — baseline
Plug in when you get home
70% of daily light vehicle energy loads between 5–10pm, mirroring commute return times. 30% charges overnight. This is how most EVs charge today — and it lands directly on top of the existing evening demand peak.

Smart charging — simulated
Shift to overnight automatically

EV adoption raises the peak.

Key finding

Ontario's smart charging result holds at 100% elimination through 2035 — because light vehicle evening load shifts entirely out of the peak window and the overnight grid has capacity to absorb it. Alberta's efficiency drops to 49% by 2035 as the EV fleet grows large enough to crowd the overnight window itself. Smart charging buys time — but Alberta will need grid investment before 2035 regardless.

GHG impact

Same cars. Same behaviour.
16× the climate impact in Alberta.

Every megawatt shifted out of peak hours avoids generation from the marginal plant — the one that fires up to meet demand spikes. In Ontario that marginal plant is often gas peaker: ~30 kg CO₂/MWh. In Alberta it's typically coal or gas: ~490 kg CO₂/MWh.

The policy implication

Smart charging mandates are often framed as a grid reliability tool. This model shows they're equally a climate tool — and a much more powerful one in provinces with fossil-heavy generation. Alberta has every incentive to prioritize smart charging infrastructure over Ontario, even though Ontario's grid stress score from Chapter 2 is higher in absolute terms. Where the grid is dirtiest, the scheduling dividend is largest.
